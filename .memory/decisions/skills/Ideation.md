# Ideation

**Tier:** Universal

## Core Principle

**Quantity enables quality.** Generate many options before judging any—the best idea often comes from the worst one.

## Problem

Brainstorming is unfocused and unproductive:
- Same ideas get recycled
- Groups converge too quickly
- Creative thinking is constrained by assumptions
- No structure leads to shallow exploration

## Solution

Structured creativity techniques that break conventional thinking patterns.

**Auto-activates when:** User mentions "brainstorm", "generate ideas", "creative", "SCAMPER", "come up with options"

## Techniques

### SCAMPER

Systematic prompts to generate ideas by modifying existing concepts. Each letter represents a different way to transform something. Invented by Bob Eberle, based on Alex Osborn's brainstorming techniques.

#### When to Use

- Improving an existing product, process, or idea
- Need structured approach to creativity
- Brainstorming feels random or unfocused
- Want comprehensive coverage of modification options
- **Time:** 20-40 minutes

#### The SCAMPER Framework

**S - Substitute:** What can be replaced with something else?
- What components can be substituted?
- What materials can be replaced?
- Who else could do this?
- What other process could we use?
- What if we changed the setting/location?

*Example (Coffee shop):* Substitute staff with automation, plastic cups with edible cups, fixed location with mobile cart.

**C - Combine:** What can be merged together?
- What can we combine with something else?
- What ideas can be merged?
- What functions can be combined?
- Can we combine resources with another team/org?

*Example (Coffee shop):* Combine with coworking space, bookstore, or laundromat.

**A - Adapt:** What can be adjusted for a different context?
- What else is this like?
- What could we copy from another industry?
- How has this problem been solved elsewhere?
- What ideas from other fields can we borrow?

*Example (Coffee shop):* Adapt subscription model from SaaS, drive-through from fast food, appointment booking from salons.

**M - Modify/Magnify/Minimize:** What can be changed in scale, form, or attributes?
- What can we make bigger/smaller?
- What can we exaggerate?
- What attributes can we enhance or reduce?
- What if we changed the shape/color/texture?

*Example (Coffee shop):* Magnify (huge cups, massive rewards), Minimize (tiny shop, 3-item menu), Modify (24/7 hours, standing-only seating).

**P - Put to Another Use:** What other purposes could this serve?
- What else could this be used for?
- Who else could use this?
- Could this solve a different problem?
- What unexpected uses exist?

*Example (Coffee shop):* Use as event venue after hours, package pickup location, meeting room rental.

**E - Eliminate:** What can be removed?
- What can we get rid of?
- What's unnecessary?
- What would happen if we removed this component?
- What's only there because of tradition?

*Example (Coffee shop):* Eliminate physical store (delivery only), menu (barista's choice), cups (bring your own).

**R - Rearrange/Reverse:** What can be reordered or inverted?
- What if we reversed the process?
- What if we changed the sequence?
- What if we swapped roles?
- What if we did this backward?

*Example (Coffee shop):* Customer makes their own coffee, pay at end instead of beginning, customers set prices.

#### Process

**Solo Process** (20-30 min):
1. Write down what you're improving (1 min)
2. Set timer for 3 min per letter (21 min)
3. For each letter: Write 3-5 ideas, no filtering
4. Review all ideas (5 min): Star the promising ones
5. Develop top 3 (5 min): Flesh out the best ideas

**Group Process** (40-60 min):
1. Define focus (5 min)
2. Individual SCAMPER (15 min): Everyone does solo
3. Share round (15 min): 2-3 ideas per letter, round-robin
4. Build on favorites (10 min): "Yes, and..." promising ideas
5. Vote and prioritize (5 min)

#### Worksheet Template

| Letter | Question | Ideas |
|--------|----------|-------|
| **S**ubstitute | What can be replaced? | 1. 2. 3. |
| **C**ombine | What can be merged? | 1. 2. 3. |
| **A**dapt | What can be borrowed? | 1. 2. 3. |
| **M**odify | What can change in scale? | 1. 2. 3. |
| **P**ut to use | What other purposes? | 1. 2. 3. |
| **E**liminate | What can be removed? | 1. 2. 3. |
| **R**earrange | What can be reordered? | 1. 2. 3. |

#### Common Mistakes

- **Evaluating while generating** - Save judgment for after
- **Staying safe** - The weird ideas are the point
- **Skipping letters** - Every letter has value, even "irrelevant" ones
- **One idea per letter** - Push for multiple options
- **Ignoring "bad" ideas** - They inform the space of possibilities

#### Tips

- **Don't skip letters** - Even if a letter seems irrelevant, force yourself to generate ideas. The "irrelevant" prompts often produce the most novel ideas.
- **Think extreme** - When stuck, go to extremes. "What if we eliminated EVERYTHING?" often unlocks thinking.
- **Borrow widely** - For "Adapt," look at completely unrelated industries. How would a hospital/airline/game company solve this?
- **Combine combinations** - After SCAMPER, look for ideas from different letters that could combine.

### Walt Disney Method

Three distinct thinking phases: Dreamer, Realist, and Critic. Named after Walt Disney's creative process, where he reportedly had three different rooms for these three modes of thinking.

#### When to Use

- Developing new concepts or projects
- Need both creativity and practicality
- Team tends to criticize ideas too early
- Want balanced evaluation of possibilities
- **Time:** 30-60 minutes

#### The Three Roles

**The Dreamer**

Goal: Generate possibilities without limits.

Mindset:
- "Anything is possible"
- "What if..."
- No constraints exist
- Wild ideas are best

Questions:
- What would be the ideal solution?
- What would we do with unlimited resources?
- What's the most exciting possibility?
- What would we do if we couldn't fail?

Rules:
- No criticism allowed
- No "that won't work"
- No practical concerns
- Build on every idea with "Yes, and..."

**The Realist**

Goal: Turn dreams into actionable plans.

Mindset:
- "How do we make this happen?"
- "What would it take?"
- Practical but still positive
- Solution-focused

Questions:
- How would we actually implement this?
- What resources would we need?
- What's the timeline?
- What are the concrete steps?

Rules:
- Take dreams seriously
- Focus on HOW, not WHETHER
- Break into concrete steps
- Stay constructive

**The Critic**

Goal: Find problems before reality does.

Mindset:
- "What could go wrong?"
- "What are we missing?"
- Constructive skepticism
- Protective, not destructive

Questions:
- What are the risks?
- What could fail?
- What assumptions are we making?
- What haven't we considered?

Rules:
- Critique the plan, not the dream
- Be specific about concerns
- End with "this would be stronger if..."
- Criticism serves the dream

#### Process

**Phase 1: Dreamer** (10-15 min)

Enter the dreamer space completely:
1. State the challenge or opportunity
2. Forget all constraints
3. Generate the most exciting possibilities
4. Aim for quantity—at least 10-15 ideas
5. Build on each other's wild ideas
6. No evaluation whatsoever

Output: List of exciting possibilities

**Phase 2: Realist** (10-15 min)

Switch to realist mode:
1. Take top 3-5 dreams from Phase 1
2. For each dream, outline implementation:
   - Key steps required
   - Resources needed
   - Timeline estimate
   - Who's involved
3. Make dreams concrete and actionable

Output: Implementation plans for top ideas

**Phase 3: Critic** (10-15 min)

Enter critic mode:
1. For each plan, identify:
   - Risks and potential failures
   - Missing elements
   - Weak assumptions
   - Stakeholder concerns
2. Be specific and constructive
3. Frame as "This would be stronger if..."
4. Don't kill ideas—strengthen them

Output: Risk assessment and improvement suggestions

**Phase 4: Integration** (5-10 min)

Return to all three perspectives:
1. How can we address the critic's concerns?
2. Does this change what's needed from the realist?
3. Does the dreamer have ideas to solve problems?
4. Synthesize into final concept

Output: Refined, stress-tested concept

#### Solo Walt Disney Method

When working alone:
1. **Physically move** between modes (different seats, rooms, or even just standing vs. sitting)
2. **Use different tools** (whiteboard for dreamer, spreadsheet for realist, red pen for critic)
3. **Complete each phase fully** before moving to next
4. **Set timers** to prevent critic from sneaking into dreamer phase
5. **Write as each persona** - actually write "As the critic, I think..."

#### Example

**Challenge:** Create a better employee onboarding experience

**Dreamer phase:**
- VR tour of the company before first day
- Personal AI assistant that knows everything about the company
- "Buddy" matching based on personality
- Gamified learning with real rewards
- Choose-your-own-adventure first week
- Direct access to CEO for Q&A

**Realist phase** (for "AI assistant" idea):
- Build on existing chatbot technology
- Feed it company wiki and policies
- Train on common new-hire questions
- Phase 1: FAQ answers (2 weeks)
- Phase 2: Personalized recommendations (4 weeks)
- Phase 3: Learning path generation (8 weeks)

**Critic phase:**
- AI might give wrong answers → Need human review loop
- New hires might rely on it too much → Balance with human connection
- Training data needs constant updating → Assign content owner
- Could feel impersonal → Combine with buddy program

**Integration:**
Final concept: AI assistant + human buddy program, where the AI handles factual questions and the buddy handles culture/relationship building. Start with simple FAQ bot to prove value before investing in advanced features.

#### Common Mistakes

- **Mixing phases** - Criticism during dreaming kills ideas
- **Skipping dreamer** - Starting "realistic" produces boring ideas
- **Destructive criticism** - "That's stupid" vs. "That would be stronger if..."
- **Unequal time** - Critic phase often goes too long
- **Not integrating** - Stopping after criticism instead of improving

#### Tips

- **Commit fully to each role** - Half-hearted dreaming produces mediocre ideas. Half-hearted criticism misses real risks.
- **Guard the transitions** - The most common failure is the critic sneaking into the dreamer phase. Facilitate transitions explicitly.
- **Dreamer first, always** - Starting with criticism kills creativity. Even if you're "naturally critical," go through dreamer phase first.
- **Return to dreamer if stuck** - If critic identifies problems with no solutions, return to dreamer mode to solve them.

### Reverse Brainstorming

Generate ideas by solving the opposite problem. Instead of "how do we improve X," ask "how do we make X worse?" Then reverse those ideas into solutions.

#### When to Use

- Stuck on a problem, can't generate ideas
- Need fresh perspective on familiar challenge
- Want to identify hidden problems
- Looking for unconventional solutions
- **Time:** 15-30 minutes

#### Why It Works

Our brains find it easier to:
- Criticize than create
- See problems than solutions
- Imagine failure than success

Reverse brainstorming harnesses this tendency. It's psychologically easier to think of ways to cause problems than to solve them.

**Bonus benefit:** The "make it worse" ideas often reveal actual problems you hadn't noticed.

#### Process

**Step 1: Define the Goal** (2 min)

State what you're trying to achieve.

Examples:
- "Improve customer satisfaction"
- "Increase employee engagement"
- "Make our product easier to use"

**Step 2: Reverse the Goal** (2 min)

Flip it to the opposite.

Reversals:
- "How do we destroy customer satisfaction?"
- "How do we maximize employee disengagement?"
- "How do we make our product impossible to use?"

**Step 3: Brainstorm the Reverse** (10-15 min)

Generate ideas for achieving the reversed goal. Be creative and thorough.

Tips:
- Think of real-world examples of bad experiences
- Consider every touchpoint
- Go extreme—the worse, the better
- Have fun with it—absurdity is welcome

Example ("How to destroy customer satisfaction"):
1. Never respond to emails
2. Make return policy impossible to find
3. Use confusing jargon everywhere
4. Charge hidden fees at checkout
5. Make support phone number a maze
6. Ship products late with no tracking
7. Send daily promotional spam
8. Make cancellation require a phone call
9. Use dark patterns everywhere
10. Blame customers for problems

**Step 4: Reverse the Ideas** (5-10 min)

Flip each bad idea into a good solution.

| Make It Worse | Make It Better |
|---------------|----------------|
| Never respond to emails | Respond within 4 hours guaranteed |
| Hidden return policy | Return policy on every product page |
| Confusing jargon | Plain language, tested with users |
| Hidden fees | All-in pricing shown upfront |
| Late shipping, no tracking | Real-time tracking, proactive updates |
| Daily spam | Preference center, email only when valuable |
| Phone-only cancellation | Self-service cancellation |
| Dark patterns | Clear, honest UX |

**Step 5: Prioritize** (5 min)

From your reversed solutions, identify:
- Quick wins (easy to implement, high impact)
- Strategic improvements (harder but valuable)
- New insights (things you hadn't considered)

#### Example: Improving Meeting Effectiveness

**Reverse goal:** "How do we make meetings as unproductive as possible?"

**Make it worse:**
1. No agenda—just show up and wing it
2. Invite everyone—all 50 people
3. Start late, run over
4. Let one person dominate
5. No clear decision maker
6. Never follow up on action items
7. Hold meetings at bad times
8. End without summarizing decisions

**Make it better:**

| Problem | Solution |
|---------|----------|
| No agenda | Agenda required 24h before, or meeting canceled |
| Invite everyone | Max 6 people, others get summary |
| Start late | Start on time regardless, late arrivals catch up |
| One dominator | Facilitator ensures all voices heard |
| No decision maker | Named decision maker for each topic |
| No follow-up | Action items tracked, reviewed at next meeting |
| Bad timing | No meetings before 10am or after 4pm |
| No summary | Last 5 min always: decisions made, actions assigned |

#### Variations

- **Reverse Reverse:** After reversing bad ideas to good, reverse the good ideas back to bad. Sometimes you find even better solutions in the second pass.
- **Competitive Reverse:** "How would our worst competitor destroy our business?" Then reverse to defensive strategies.
- **Process Reverse:** "How do we make this process take 10x longer and cost 10x more?" Reveals efficiency opportunities.
- **Culture Reverse:** "How do we create the most toxic workplace possible?" Surfaces cultural risks and improvements.

#### Common Mistakes

- **Not going negative enough** - Safe "bad ideas" don't produce insights
- **Skipping the reverse** - Getting stuck on problems without solutions
- **Taking it too seriously** - This should be fun and energizing
- **Ignoring revelations** - Sometimes the "bad ideas" reveal actual current problems
- **Weak reversals** - "Don't do that" isn't a solution—be specific about what TO do

#### Tips

- **Go extreme** - "Make it slightly worse" produces boring ideas. "Make it catastrophically, hilariously worse" produces insights.
- **Draw from experience** - Think about your worst experiences as a customer, employee, user. What made them terrible?
- **Look for patterns** - If multiple "make it worse" ideas cluster around a theme, that's a weak area to prioritize.
