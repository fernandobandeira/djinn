# Role Playing

**Tier:** Foundational

## Core Principle

**Step into the role to see what you're missing.** Role playing is the foundation of empathy, critique, and creative thinking—use it to unlock any skill that requires perspective-shifting.

## Problem

Single perspective misses important viewpoints:
- Stakeholders feel unheard
- Decisions ignore key constituencies
- Solutions optimize for one group at others' expense
- Hidden concerns surface too late

## Solution

Multi-perspective techniques that systematically explore different viewpoints.

**Auto-activates when:** User mentions "perspectives", "what would X think", "six hats", "stakeholder view", "different viewpoints"

## Techniques

### Six Thinking Hats

Examine ideas through six distinct thinking modes. Created by Edward de Bono, this technique separates different types of thinking so each gets full attention.

#### When to Use

- Need structured exploration of an idea
- Group keeps mixing emotions with facts
- Want to ensure creative AND critical thinking
- Decision feels stuck or circular
- **Time:** 30-60 minutes for full cycle

#### The Six Hats

**White Hat - Facts & Information**

Pure data. What do we know? What don't we know?

Focus on:
- Available data and facts
- Information gaps
- How to get missing information
- Trends and numbers

Questions:
- "What facts do we have?"
- "What data is missing?"
- "How reliable is this information?"
- "What would we need to verify?"

Avoid: Opinions, interpretations, feelings

**Red Hat - Emotions & Intuition**

Gut feelings without justification required.

Focus on:
- Immediate emotional reactions
- Intuitions and hunches
- Likes and dislikes
- Fears and excitement

Questions:
- "What's your gut reaction?"
- "How does this make you feel?"
- "What excites or worries you?"
- "What does your intuition say?"

Note: No need to explain or justify. "I don't like it" is valid.

**Black Hat - Critical Judgment**

Devil's advocate. What could go wrong?

Focus on:
- Risks and dangers
- Weaknesses and flaws
- Why it might not work
- Logical problems

Questions:
- "What could go wrong?"
- "What are the weaknesses?"
- "Why might this fail?"
- "What doesn't fit?"

Avoid: Being negative about the process itself

**Yellow Hat - Optimism & Benefits**

Best-case thinking. What's the value?

Focus on:
- Benefits and advantages
- Value and opportunities
- Why it could work
- Best possible outcomes

Questions:
- "What are the benefits?"
- "Why is this valuable?"
- "What opportunities does this create?"
- "What's the best case scenario?"

Note: Must be logically grounded, not wishful thinking

**Green Hat - Creativity & Alternatives**

New ideas. What else is possible?

Focus on:
- New ideas and possibilities
- Alternatives and variations
- Creative modifications
- "What if" exploration

Questions:
- "What other approaches exist?"
- "How could we modify this?"
- "What if we tried...?"
- "What's a completely different way?"

Note: Quantity over quality. No judgment during green hat.

**Blue Hat - Process Control**

Meta-thinking. Managing the thinking process itself.

Focus on:
- Organizing the thinking
- Summarizing progress
- Deciding next steps
- Ensuring all hats get time

Questions:
- "What hat should we use now?"
- "Have we covered all perspectives?"
- "What conclusions are emerging?"
- "What's our next step?"

#### Process

**Setup:**
1. State the topic/decision clearly
2. Explain each hat briefly
3. Set time limit per hat (5-10 minutes)
4. Designate someone to track insights

**Sequence Options:**

| Sequence | Use For |
|----------|---------|
| Blue → White → Green → Yellow → Black → Red → Blue | Standard (new topics) |
| Blue → White → Black → Green → Yellow → Red → Blue | Problem-solving |
| White → Yellow → Black → Red | Quick decisions |

**Facilitation Tips:**
- **Everyone wears same hat** - No mixing perspectives
- **Timebox strictly** - Move on even if not "done"
- **No hat-mixing** - "That's a black hat thought, save it"
- **Document per hat** - Keep insights organized
- **Return to hats** - It's okay to revisit

#### Example

**Topic:** Should we open-source our internal tool?

**White Hat** (Facts):
- Tool has 50 internal users
- Similar tools exist (list them)
- Would require documentation effort
- No competitor intelligence concerns

**Red Hat** (Feelings):
- Excited about community building
- Worried about support burden
- Pride in sharing our work
- Anxiety about code quality judgment

**Black Hat** (Risks):
- Support requests could overwhelm team
- Competitors could benefit
- Might expose security issues
- Documentation is currently poor

**Yellow Hat** (Benefits):
- Attracts talent who use the tool
- Community might contribute improvements
- Builds company reputation
- Forces us to improve code quality

**Green Hat** (Alternatives):
- Open-source core, keep extensions private
- Start with "source available" license
- Partner with existing open-source project
- Create a simplified version for public

**Blue Hat** (Synthesis):
- Key tension: support burden vs. reputation benefits
- Need more data on similar open-source projects' experience
- Decision: Pilot with limited release, measure support load

#### Solo Use

You can use six hats alone:
1. Write the topic at top of page
2. Create six sections
3. Spend 5 minutes per hat
4. Force yourself to stay in mode
5. Synthesize at the end

The discipline of separating thinking modes is valuable even solo.

#### Common Mistakes

- **Mixing hats** - Jumping between thinking modes
- **Skipping red hat** - Emotions influence everything, better to surface them
- **Black hat dominance** - Some groups over-index on criticism
- **Rushing green hat** - Creativity needs time and safety
- **Forgetting blue hat** - Process management is crucial

### Stakeholder Roundtable

Role-play as different stakeholders to surface hidden concerns, conflicting interests, and overlooked perspectives. Imagine each stakeholder at the table, advocating for their needs.

#### When to Use

- Decision affects multiple groups differently
- Need to anticipate reactions before announcing
- Want to find solutions that work for everyone
- Stakeholders have conflicting interests
- **Time:** 20-40 minutes

#### Process

**Step 1: Identify Stakeholders**

List everyone affected by or influencing the decision.

Common stakeholder categories:
- Direct users / customers
- Internal teams (engineering, sales, support, etc.)
- Leadership / management
- Partners / vendors
- Regulators / compliance
- Community / public

Questions to find hidden stakeholders:
- "Who will be affected by this?"
- "Who could block this?"
- "Who needs to approve this?"
- "Who will have to change their behavior?"
- "Who benefits? Who loses?"

**Step 2: Define Each Stakeholder**

For each stakeholder, establish:

| Attribute | Question |
|-----------|----------|
| **Goals** | What do they want to achieve? |
| **Concerns** | What are they worried about? |
| **Constraints** | What limits them? |
| **Power** | How much influence do they have? |
| **Success metric** | How do they measure good outcomes? |

**Step 3: Run the Roundtable**

For each stakeholder, adopt their perspective fully:

Opening prompt:
> "Speaking as [stakeholder], what do you think about [topic]?"

Dig deeper:
- "What's your biggest concern?"
- "What would make this work for you?"
- "What would make you reject this?"
- "What information do you need?"
- "What would you trade off?"

**Stay in character** - Advocate genuinely for that stakeholder's interests.

**Step 4: Map Conflicts and Alignments**

After hearing all perspectives:

| Stakeholder A | Stakeholder B | Relationship |
|---------------|---------------|--------------|
| Engineering | Product | Aligned on X, conflict on Y |
| Users | Sales | Conflict on pricing model |

Key conflicts to resolve:
- List the genuine tensions between stakeholders
- Note which conflicts are fundamental vs. resolvable

**Step 5: Find Synthesis**

Look for solutions that address multiple stakeholder needs:
- "What would satisfy both X and Y?"
- "What's the minimum viable for each stakeholder?"
- "Where can we sequence to address concerns over time?"
- "What trade-offs are acceptable to which stakeholders?"

#### Stakeholder Matrix Template

| Stakeholder | Goals | Concerns | Power | Position |
|-------------|-------|----------|-------|----------|
| [Name] | [What they want] | [What worries them] | H/M/L | Support/Neutral/Oppose |

#### Example

**Topic:** Switching from monthly to annual billing

**Customer perspective:**
> "I'm worried about committing for a full year. What if my needs change? I want flexibility. If you force annual, I might look elsewhere. But if there's a significant discount, I'd consider it."

**Sales perspective:**
> "Annual contracts give me predictable revenue and reduce churn. I can offer better pricing. But some customers will push back hard—I need tools to handle objections and maybe a monthly option for SMB."

**Finance perspective:**
> "Annual billing improves cash flow and revenue predictability. We can plan better. But we need to handle the accounting complexity of deferred revenue. Also concerned about refund policies."

**Support perspective:**
> "Annual customers have higher expectations. If they're locked in and unhappy, support burden increases. We need better onboarding to prevent issues. Also, billing date confusion is a major ticket driver."

**Synthesis:**
- Offer both options (annual with discount, monthly with premium)
- Improve onboarding for annual customers
- Create clear refund/cancellation policy
- Give sales objection-handling training
- Solve billing date UX issues first

#### Solo Variation

When working alone:
1. Write each stakeholder's name on a separate page/section
2. Physically move or change position for each perspective
3. Write in first person as that stakeholder
4. Don't skip stakeholders you find annoying
5. Synthesize only after all perspectives are captured

#### Common Mistakes

- **Straw-manning** - Making stakeholders' views artificially weak
- **Skipping uncomfortable perspectives** - The CEO, the angry customer, the regulator
- **Rushing to synthesis** - Let tensions exist before resolving
- **Assuming you know** - Even stakeholders you work with daily can surprise you
- **Forgetting power dynamics** - Some stakeholders matter more for decisions

#### Tips

- **Commit to the perspective** - Don't just list what they'd think—actually argue for it. Get a little uncomfortable advocating for views you don't hold.
- **Include difficult stakeholders** - The stakeholder you'd rather not think about often has the insight you need.
- **Note emotional reactions** - "As this stakeholder, I feel frustrated/excited/anxious because..."
- **Challenge obvious alignment** - Even stakeholders who "should" agree often have subtle conflicts.

### Persona Analysis

Test ideas against specific user personas to ensure solutions work for real people, not abstract "users." Each persona represents a distinct user type with their own goals, frustrations, and context.

#### When to Use

- Designing features or experiences
- Validating that solutions fit actual users
- Identifying gaps in user coverage
- Building empathy for different user types
- **Time:** 15-30 minutes per persona

#### Process

**Step 1: Define Personas**

Create 3-5 distinct personas representing your key user types.

Persona template:
```
Name: [Memorable name that captures essence]
Role: [What they do]
Goal: [Primary objective]
Frustration: [Main pain point]
Context: [Relevant background]
Quote: [Something they'd actually say]
```

Persona differentiation criteria:
- Technical skill level
- Frequency of use
- Primary use case
- Decision-making power
- Risk tolerance
- Resource constraints

**Step 2: Validate Persona Distinctness**

Good personas should:
- Have genuinely different needs
- React differently to the same feature
- Require different communication styles
- Have non-overlapping primary goals

**Red flag:** If all your personas would react the same way, they're not distinct enough.

**Step 3: Test Ideas Against Each Persona**

For each persona, evaluate your idea:

Core questions:
- "Would [Persona] use this? Why or why not?"
- "What would [Persona] find confusing?"
- "What's missing for [Persona]?"
- "How would [Persona] discover this?"
- "What would make [Persona] recommend this?"

Scenario walkthrough:
> "It's Tuesday morning. [Persona] opens the product because [trigger]. They want to [goal]. Walk through what happens."

**Step 4: Identify Coverage Gaps**

After testing all personas:

| Persona | Works for them? | What's missing? | Priority |
|---------|-----------------|-----------------|----------|
| [Name] | Yes/Partial/No | [Gap] | H/M/L |

Questions:
- Which personas are underserved?
- Are we over-optimizing for one persona?
- Who would we lose with this design?
- What's the minimum to serve each persona?

**Step 5: Prioritize and Adapt**

Decide how to handle persona conflicts:
- **Primary persona:** Optimize for them, acceptable for others
- **Must-serve:** All personas need baseline experience
- **Defer:** Some personas can wait for future iteration

#### Example Personas

**For a project management tool:**

**Persona 1: "Startup Sam"**
- Role: Solo founder wearing all hats
- Goal: Get things out of my head quickly
- Frustration: Tools that require setup before value
- Context: Uses phone often, works irregular hours
- Quote: "I don't have time to learn your system"

**Persona 2: "Manager Maria"**
- Role: Team lead with 8 direct reports
- Goal: Know what everyone's working on
- Frustration: Chasing people for status updates
- Context: In meetings half the day, needs async visibility
- Quote: "Just tell me if we're on track"

**Persona 3: "Process Pete"**
- Role: PMO lead at enterprise company
- Goal: Standardize workflows across teams
- Frustration: Teams doing things inconsistently
- Context: Needs reporting, compliance, audit trails
- Quote: "How do I enforce this across 200 people?"

**Testing a feature: "AI task suggestions"**

**Startup Sam:** "Maybe useful? But don't slow me down with suggestions when I'm dumping tasks. Let me turn it off easily."

**Manager Maria:** "Could help with task assignment. But I need to trust it won't suggest wrong things to my team. Can I review before it suggests to others?"

**Process Pete:** "Interesting for productivity metrics. But how does it handle our custom fields? Can it follow our naming conventions? What data is it trained on?"

#### Persona Creation Workshop Format

If creating personas from scratch:

1. **Gather inputs** (30 min)
   - User research, support tickets, sales notes
   - List distinct user behaviors observed

2. **Cluster behaviors** (15 min)
   - Group similar users together
   - Name each cluster

3. **Draft personas** (20 min)
   - Fill out template for each cluster
   - Add distinguishing details

4. **Validate distinctness** (10 min)
   - Test: Would they react differently to [feature]?
   - Merge or split as needed

5. **Prioritize** (10 min)
   - Which personas matter most?
   - Who's primary vs. secondary?

#### Common Mistakes

- **Too many personas** - 3-5 is manageable, 10 is unusable
- **Demographic-only** - Age/gender matter less than goals/behaviors
- **Unrealistic personas** - Based on imagination rather than research
- **Static personas** - Never updated with new learnings
- **Ignored personas** - Created but never actually used in decisions

#### Tips

- **Make personas specific** - "Small business owner" is too vague. "Restaurant owner with 3 locations, 20 employees, and no IT staff" is actionable.
- **Include edge personas** - The power user who pushes limits. The reluctant user forced to use it. The administrator who never uses the product directly.
- **Update personas with research** - Personas should evolve as you learn more about real users. Static personas become stale.
- **Use real quotes when possible** - Nothing beats actual user quotes for grounding personas in reality.
