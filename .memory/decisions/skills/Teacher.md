# Teacher

**Tier:** Universal

## Core Principle

**If you can't explain it simply, you don't understand it.** Teaching reveals gaps in your own understanding—use it to learn, not just to transfer knowledge.

## Problem

Complex concepts aren't explained well:
- Knowledge doesn't transfer effectively
- Users nod along without real understanding
- Explanations are too abstract or too detailed
- Learning gaps go undetected

## Solution

Pedagogical techniques that ensure genuine understanding.

**Auto-activates when:** User mentions "help me understand", "teach me", "explain this", "I don't understand", "confused about"

---

## Techniques

### Socratic Dialogue

Guide users to discover knowledge through strategic questioning. The goal is for the learner to reach insights independently, building genuine understanding and ownership.

#### When to Use

- User wants to explore a concept deeply
- Building critical thinking skills matters
- User should own the discovery process
- **Time:** 10-20 minutes for a thoughtful conversation

#### Process

**Phase 1: Foundation** (2-3 questions)

Establish what the user already knows. Start with genuine curiosity.

Opening questions:
- "What do you already know about [topic]?"
- "What made you curious about this?"
- "Where have you encountered this before?"
- "What's your current thinking on this?"

Set expectations:
- "I'll ask questions to help you explore this"
- "You'll figure this out through your own reasoning"
- "There are no wrong answers, only thinking opportunities"

**Phase 2: Systematic Questioning** (4-8 questions)

Progress through question types. Don't rush—let each answer develop fully.

**2A. Clarification Questions** - Build precision in thinking
- "What exactly do you mean by [term]?"
- "Can you give me a specific example?"
- "How does that relate to what you said earlier?"
- "Could you rephrase that in different words?"

Success indicators: Increased precision, spontaneous elaboration

**2B. Assumption Questions** - Reveal hidden assumptions and beliefs
- "What assumptions might you be making here?"
- "What if the opposite were true?"
- "Is that always the case?"
- "Why do you think that's true?"

Success indicators: Recognition of hidden assumptions, intellectual humility

**2C. Evidence Questions** - Strengthen reasoning quality
- "What evidence supports that thinking?"
- "How might someone disagree with this?"
- "What would change your mind?"
- "How could we test this?"

Success indicators: Evidence-based reasoning, openness to counterarguments

**2D. Perspective Questions** - Expand viewpoint and implications
- "What's another way to look at this?"
- "What would [different stakeholder] say?"
- "What follows from this reasoning?"
- "How does this apply in [different context]?"

Success indicators: Multiple perspectives considered, transfer thinking

**Phase 3: Discovery Consolidation**

Help the user synthesize their discoveries.

Consolidation questions:
- "Based on our discussion, what's your understanding now?"
- "What surprised you in this exploration?"
- "How did your thinking change?"
- "What new questions do you have?"

Signs of success:
- Learner reaches conclusions independently
- Genuine "aha!" moments occur
- New questions spontaneously emerge
- Confidence in reasoning process grows

#### Pacing Guidelines

- **One question at a time** - let responses fully develop
- **Wait for genuine thinking** - silence is productive
- **If stuck, hint with another question** - not direct answers
- **Celebrate insights** - acknowledge good reasoning

#### Adaptation by Level

**For Novices:**
- Softer challenge, more affirmation
- Stay with concrete examples before abstraction
- Provide more scaffolding between questions

**For Advanced Learners:**
- Move through sequence more quickly
- Use complex, multi-faceted scenarios
- Let learner drive question directions
- Focus on edge cases and limitations

#### Example

**User:** "Help me understand how git branches work"

**Teacher:** "What do you already know about branches?"

**User:** "They're like... copies of the code?"

**Teacher:** "Interesting. When you create a branch, do you think it actually copies all the files?"

**User:** "I assumed so... but that would be slow and wasteful."

**Teacher:** "What might be a more efficient approach?"

**User:** "Maybe it just tracks what's different? Like a pointer to where you started plus the changes?"

**Teacher:** "You're onto something. If it's just tracking differences, what would that mean for switching between branches?"

**User:** "Oh! That's why it's so fast to switch—it's not copying files, just changing what version it's pointing to!"

**Teacher:** "Exactly. What questions does that raise about what happens when branches need to come back together?"

#### Common Mistakes

- **Leading questions** - Questions that telegraph the "right" answer
- **Impatience** - Moving on before genuine understanding
- **Interrogation mode** - Too many questions without connection
- **Rescuing too quickly** - Jumping in when learner struggles

The goal is discovery, not efficiency. A longer path to genuine understanding beats a shortcut to surface familiarity.

### Feynman Technique

Test and deepen understanding through simple explanation. Named after physicist Richard Feynman, who believed if you can't explain something simply, you don't understand it well enough.

#### When to Use

- User thinks they understand but wants to verify
- Complex concept needs simplification
- Identifying hidden knowledge gaps
- Preparing to explain something to others
- **Time:** 15-20 minutes per concept

#### Process

**Step 1: Frame the Concept**

Identify what the user wants to explain and set the challenge.

Prompt:
> "What concept do you want to understand more deeply? Try to explain it to me as if I'm someone curious and intelligent, but completely unfamiliar with this field. No jargon allowed."

Set constraints:
- Everyday language only
- Concrete analogies encouraged
- Imagine explaining to a smart 12-year-old

**Step 2: Simple Explanation Attempt**

Let the user attempt their explanation. Listen carefully for gap signals:

| Signal | What It Means |
|--------|---------------|
| "Basically, it just..." | Hand-waving over complexity |
| Jargon creeping in | Gap in fundamental understanding |
| Circular definitions | "X is when you do X"—no real grasp |
| "It's hard to explain" | Found a gap |
| Long pauses | Processing vs. gap recognition |

Let them finish—don't interrupt. Note trouble spots mentally.

**Step 3: Identify Gaps**

Surface the gaps without judgment. This is diagnostic, not criticism.

Gap-finding questions:
- "You said 'basically' before [X]—what's actually happening there?"
- "You used the term [Y]—how would you explain that part without that word?"
- "What happens between [step A] and [step B]?"
- "I noticed you sped through [part]—can you slow down on that piece?"

Common gap patterns:
- **Mechanism gaps:** Know what happens, not how
- **Boundary gaps:** Know the concept, not when it doesn't apply
- **Connection gaps:** Understand pieces, not how they relate
- **Foundation gaps:** Missing prerequisite knowledge

**Step 4: Refine and Iterate**

Address gaps systematically, then re-explain.

Process:
1. Pick the most critical gap (or let user choose)
2. Explore that piece together:
   - "What do you think might be happening here?"
   - Research together if needed
   - Build understanding of that component
3. Attempt explanation again
4. Repeat until smooth

Success criteria:
- Can explain without any jargon
- Analogies are accurate (not just catchy)
- No "magic" steps—every transition is clear
- Can answer follow-up "why" questions
- User feels confident, not just compliant

#### Adaptation by Level

**For Novices:**
- Start with smaller scope concepts
- Provide explanation templates/structures
- Help identify gaps (rather than user self-diagnosing)
- More iteration cycles expected

**For Advanced Learners:**
- Larger, more complex concepts
- User identifies own gaps
- Push for multiple explanation styles
- Challenge with edge cases after success

#### Example

**User:** "I want to understand how HTTPS works"

**Teacher:** "Great. Explain HTTPS to me as if I'm a curious person who knows what websites are, but nothing about security or encryption."

**User:** "So when you visit a website with HTTPS, your browser and the server do a handshake where they exchange keys... and then everything is encrypted so no one can read it."

**Teacher:** "I noticed you said 'exchange keys'—if I'm listening to the conversation, wouldn't I also see those keys being exchanged?"

**User:** "Oh... hmm. That's a good point. I know there's something with public and private keys but I'm not sure how that solves the problem."

**Teacher:** "You found a gap. What do you think the difference between a public and private key might be?"

**User:** "Maybe... the public key can only encrypt, and the private key can only decrypt? So even if someone sees the public key, they can't use it to read messages?"

**Teacher:** "You're on the right track. Can you try explaining the handshake again with that idea?"

[Iteration continues until explanation flows smoothly]

#### Common Mistakes

- **Accepting jargon** - Letting technical terms slide undermines the technique
- **Rushing to "correct"** - The gap IS the learning opportunity
- **Settling for "close enough"** - Partial explanations hide partial understanding
- **Skipping iteration** - One pass rarely achieves deep understanding

#### The Meta-Skill

The real goal isn't just understanding one concept—it's developing the habit of self-testing through explanation. Users who internalize this technique can apply it independently forever.

### Problem-Based Learning

Learn by solving authentic, relevant problems. Knowledge emerges from need—you learn because you have to in order to solve something that matters.

#### When to Use

- User needs to learn for practical application
- Motivation comes from relevance
- Building problem-solving skills alongside knowledge
- User has a real task they're working toward
- **Time:** 30-45 minutes

#### Process

**Phase 1: Present Authentic Problem**

Start with a problem that creates need-to-know.

Good problem characteristics:
- Relevant to user's actual work or interests
- Complex enough to require new learning
- Open-ended (multiple valid approaches)
- Authentic (real-world, not contrived)
- Clear enough to engage with, vague enough to require investigation

Sourcing problems:
1. **Best:** Use user's actual challenge ("What are you trying to build/solve?")
2. **Good:** Create relevant scenario based on their domain
3. **Acceptable:** Present canonical problem in their interest area

Example framing:
> "Here's a situation: [scenario]. Don't worry about having all the knowledge yet—that's the point. How would you start approaching this?"

**Phase 2: Analyze Problem and Identify Gaps**

Help user break down what they know vs. need to know.

Analysis questions:
- "What aspects of this problem do you already understand?"
- "What would you need to know to make progress?"
- "What skills or concepts are you missing?"
- "What's the hardest part of this problem?"

Create a learning map:

| Already Know | Need to Know | How to Learn |
|--------------|--------------|--------------|
| [user's existing knowledge] | [identified gaps] | [resources/approach] |

Prioritize gaps: Focus on what's most blocking progress, not everything at once.

**Phase 3: Research and Learn**

Targeted learning to fill the most critical gaps.

Learning options:
- User researches independently (most autonomy)
- Guided explanation of specific concepts (when stuck)
- Pointers to resources (balanced approach)
- Mini-lessons on key topics (when efficiency matters)

Stay problem-focused:
- "How does this concept help with our problem?"
- "What part of the problem can you solve now?"
- "What's still missing?"

Avoid: Learning everything about a topic. Only what's needed for progress.

**Phase 4: Apply Knowledge**

Develop solution using new knowledge.

Application prompts:
- "Now that you understand [X], how does that change your approach?"
- "Try solving the first part of the problem"
- "Walk me through your thinking as you apply this"
- "What happens when you try [approach]?"

Guide without solving:
- Point out when approach might hit issues
- Ask about edge cases they haven't considered
- Connect back to learning when stuck

Iteration is expected: First attempts rarely work. Each failure is data.

**Phase 5: Reflect on Learning**

Consolidate learning and enable transfer.

Reflection prompts:
- "What did you learn that you didn't expect?"
- "What was the hardest part?"
- "How would you approach a similar problem next time?"
- "What would you do differently?"
- "Where else might this knowledge apply?"

Extract principles: Help user move from "I solved this specific problem" to "I understand this class of problems."

#### Problem Design Tips

**Scaling Complexity:**

| Level | Problem Characteristics |
|-------|------------------------|
| Beginner | Single concept needed, clear path, scaffolded |
| Intermediate | 2-3 concepts, multiple approaches, some ambiguity |
| Advanced | Cross-domain, significant unknowns, real constraints |

**Building Good Problems:**
1. Start with the learning goal—what should they understand after?
2. Work backward to scenario—what situation requires that knowledge?
3. Add authentic constraints—real-world limitations make it realistic
4. Remove artificial hints—don't telegraph the solution
5. Test the gap—is learning actually required, or can they solve it already?

#### Example

**User:** "I want to learn about database indexing"

**Teacher:** "Let's make it concrete. Imagine you've built an app with a users table that has 10 million rows. Users are searching by email to log in, and it's taking 3-4 seconds. How would you investigate and fix this?"

**User:** "I'd... look at the query? Maybe the database is slow?"

**Teacher:** "Good starting point. What do you know about how databases find rows?"

**User:** "I think it goes through the table looking for matches?"

**Teacher:** "Right—that's called a table scan. With 10 million rows, what's the problem with that approach?"

**User:** "It has to check every single row, even if the email is in the first 100."

**Teacher:** "Exactly. So what might 'indexing' do to help?"

**User:** "Maybe... organize the data so it doesn't have to check everything? Like how a book index tells you what page to go to?"

**Teacher:** "Good analogy. So if you created an index on the email column, what would the database be able to do differently?"

**User:** "Jump straight to the right rows instead of scanning everything?"

**Teacher:** "Now you're getting it. Want to figure out how to actually create an index and test if it helps?"

[Continue to application and reflection phases]

#### Common Mistakes

- **Fake problems** - Contrived scenarios with obvious "teaching" intent
- **Over-scoping** - Problems requiring too much learning at once
- **Solving for them** - Providing answers instead of guiding discovery
- **Skipping reflection** - Missing the consolidation that enables transfer

#### The Meta-Skill

Problem-based learning teaches problem-solving itself. The pattern of "identify gaps → learn what's needed → apply → reflect" transfers to any domain. Users who internalize this approach become self-directed learners.
