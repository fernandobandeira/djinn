# Strategic Analysis

**Tier:** Domain

## Core Principle

**See the whole board before moving.** Strategic decisions require understanding all forces at play—your position, competitors, and possible futures.

## Problem

Strategic decisions lack structured frameworks:
- Important factors get missed
- Competitive dynamics aren't understood
- Future scenarios aren't explored
- Decisions made without full context

## Solution

Strategic frameworks that ensure comprehensive analysis.

**Auto-activates when:** User mentions "SWOT", "competitive forces", "scenario planning", "strategic assessment", "market forces"

## Techniques

### SWOT Analysis

Structured framework to assess Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, and Threats. Maps internal capabilities against external environment to inform strategic decisions.

#### When to Use

- Evaluating a new initiative or product
- Strategic planning sessions
- Assessing competitive position
- Deciding whether to pursue an opportunity
- Team alignment on current situation
- **Time:** 30-60 minutes

#### The Framework

```
                    HELPFUL              HARMFUL
                    (to objective)       (to objective)
              ┌─────────────────────┬─────────────────────┐
   INTERNAL   │                     │                     │
   (within    │     STRENGTHS       │     WEAKNESSES      │
   control)   │                     │                     │
              ├─────────────────────┼─────────────────────┤
   EXTERNAL   │                     │                     │
   (outside   │    OPPORTUNITIES    │       THREATS       │
   control)   │                     │                     │
              └─────────────────────┴─────────────────────┘
```

**Strengths** (Internal, Helpful) - What do we do well?
- What are we better at than competitors?
- What unique resources do we have?
- What do others see as our strengths?
- What expertise or capabilities are distinctive?

**Weaknesses** (Internal, Harmful) - What could we improve?
- What do competitors do better?
- What resources are we lacking?
- Where do we lose to competitors?
- What's not working well?

**Opportunities** (External, Helpful) - What external factors could we exploit?
- What trends could benefit us?
- What market changes create openings?
- What are competitors neglecting?
- What new technologies enable us?

**Threats** (External, Harmful) - What external factors could harm us?
- What trends could hurt us?
- What are competitors doing?
- What regulatory risks exist?
- What could make us obsolete?

#### Process

**Solo Process** (30 min):
1. Define the focus (2 min): What decision/situation are you analyzing?
2. Brainstorm each quadrant (5 min each, 20 min total): List 5-10 items per quadrant
3. Prioritize (5 min): Mark top 3 in each quadrant
4. Develop strategies (3 min): How do strengths address threats? How do opportunities offset weaknesses?

**Group Process** (60 min):
1. Align on objective (5 min): What are we analyzing?
2. Individual brainstorm (10 min): Everyone fills their own SWOT
3. Share and consolidate (20 min): Combine into master SWOT
4. Vote on priorities (10 min): Each person votes for top 3 per quadrant
5. Strategy discussion (15 min): Develop strategic responses

#### SWOT-to-Strategy Matrix

After completing SWOT, develop strategies at intersections:

| | Strengths | Weaknesses |
|---|---|---|
| **Opportunities** | **SO Strategies**: Use strengths to capture opportunities | **WO Strategies**: Address weaknesses to capture opportunities |
| **Threats** | **ST Strategies**: Use strengths to counter threats | **WT Strategies**: Minimize weaknesses and avoid threats |

**Example Strategies:**
- **SO:** "Our strong engineering team (S) can build features for the emerging API-first market (O)"
- **WO:** "Partner with a marketing firm (address W) to capitalize on growing market demand (O)"
- **ST:** "Our brand reputation (S) helps us retain customers despite new competitor (T)"
- **WT:** "Outsource non-core functions (minimize W) to reduce exposure to cost pressures (T)"

#### Common Mistakes

- **Listing facts, not insights** - "We have 50 employees" isn't a strength without context
- **Confusing internal/external** - "Market is growing" is opportunity (external), not strength (internal)
- **Wishful thinking** - Listing desired state, not actual state
- **Unbalanced analysis** - 15 strengths, 2 weaknesses = bias, not reality
- **No prioritization** - Long lists without focus on what matters most
- **Analysis stops at the matrix** - SWOT should lead to strategy, not just awareness

#### Tips

- **Be specific** - "Strong team" is too vague. "3 engineers with ML expertise" is actionable.
- **Get outside perspective** - Your blind spots are real. Ask customers, partners, outsiders.
- **Challenge categorization** - Is something really a strength, or are you assuming? Test against evidence.
- **Don't ignore the uncomfortable** - Weaknesses and threats are often understated. Push for honesty.

### Porter's Five Forces

Analyze the competitive forces that shape industry profitability and strategic positioning. Developed by Michael Porter at Harvard Business School.

#### When to Use

- Entering a new market or industry
- Assessing industry attractiveness
- Understanding why margins are high/low
- Identifying strategic leverage points
- Competitive strategy development
- **Time:** 45-90 minutes

#### The Five Forces

```
                         THREAT OF
                        NEW ENTRANTS
                             │
                             ▼
    BARGAINING    ┌─────────────────────┐    BARGAINING
    POWER OF  ───▶│  INDUSTRY RIVALRY   │◀─── POWER OF
    SUPPLIERS     │  (Existing          │     BUYERS
                  │   Competition)      │
                  └─────────────────────┘
                             ▲
                             │
                         THREAT OF
                        SUBSTITUTES
```

**Force 1: Industry Rivalry** - How intensely do existing competitors compete?

High rivalry when:
- Many competitors of similar size
- Slow industry growth
- High fixed costs (pressure to fill capacity)
- Low differentiation (compete on price)
- High exit barriers

Assessment questions:
- How many direct competitors exist?
- Is the industry growing, stable, or declining?
- How differentiated are offerings?
- How often do price wars occur?

**Force 2: Threat of New Entrants** - How easy is it for new players to enter?

Barriers to entry:
- **Economies of scale:** Large players have cost advantages
- **Capital requirements:** High investment needed to compete
- **Switching costs:** Customers locked into existing solutions
- **Proprietary technology:** Patents, trade secrets, know-how
- **Government policy:** Licenses, regulations, standards
- **Brand identity:** Established trust and recognition

**Force 3: Threat of Substitutes** - What alternatives could customers switch to?

Substitutes are threatening when:
- Price-performance is attractive
- Switching costs are low
- Buyers are willing to substitute
- Substitute is improving rapidly

Examples: Video conferencing substitutes for business travel. Streaming substitutes for cable TV. AI tools substitute for various professional services.

**Force 4: Bargaining Power of Suppliers** - How much leverage do suppliers have?

Suppliers are powerful when:
- Few suppliers (concentrated)
- No substitutes for their input
- Your industry isn't important to them
- High switching costs between suppliers
- Suppliers could integrate forward

**Force 5: Bargaining Power of Buyers** - How much leverage do customers have?

Buyers are powerful when:
- Few buyers or large volume purchases
- Products are undifferentiated
- Switching costs are low
- Buyer could integrate backward
- Buyer has full information

#### Process (60-90 min)

1. **Define industry boundaries** (10 min): What industry are you analyzing? Where do you draw the lines?
2. **Analyze each force** (10 min each, 50 min): Rate Strong (5) to Weak (1), list specific factors, identify trends
3. **Synthesize** (15 min): Which forces are most significant? Where are the profit pools?
4. **Strategic implications** (15 min): How can we position against forces? Which forces can we influence?

#### Strategic Responses

| Force | Response Options |
|-------|------------------|
| High Rivalry | Differentiate, focus on niche, acquire competitors |
| New Entrants | Raise barriers, build brand, lock in customers |
| Substitutes | Improve value, match substitute benefits, acquire |
| Supplier Power | Diversify suppliers, integrate backward, partner |
| Buyer Power | Differentiate, find less powerful segments, lock-in |

#### Common Mistakes

- **Too broad industry definition** - "Technology" isn't an industry
- **Ignoring indirect substitutes** - Don't just look at direct competitors
- **Static analysis** - Forces change—analyze trends
- **Forgetting the "so what"** - Analysis should drive strategy
- **Confusing forces** - Rivalry is existing competitors, entrants are potential

### Scenario Planning

Develop multiple plausible futures to stress-test strategies and prepare for uncertainty. Originally developed at Shell Oil in the 1970s.

Don't predict the future—prepare for multiple futures.

#### When to Use

- High uncertainty about the future
- Long-term strategic planning
- Major investment decisions
- Industry disruption possible
- Need strategies robust across futures
- **Time:** 2-4 hours (full process)

#### The Core Idea

Scenarios are NOT:
- Forecasts (single prediction)
- Wishes (desired future)
- Best/worst cases (too simplistic)

Scenarios ARE:
- Plausible alternative futures
- Internally consistent stories
- Tools for stress-testing strategies

#### The 2x2 Scenario Matrix

Identify two critical uncertainties and create four scenarios:

```
                    Uncertainty A
                    High ←────────────→ Low
              ┌─────────────────┬─────────────────┐
    High      │   Scenario 1    │   Scenario 2    │
Uncertainty   ├─────────────────┼─────────────────┤
    B   Low   │   Scenario 3    │   Scenario 4    │
              └─────────────────┴─────────────────┘
```

#### Process

**Step 1: Define the Focal Question** (15 min)

What decision or strategy are you testing?

Good focal questions:
- "What should our product strategy be for the next 5 years?"
- "Should we expand into market X?"
- "How should we position against potential disruption?"

**Step 2: Identify Driving Forces** (30 min)

List forces that could shape the future:
- Social/demographic trends
- Technology changes
- Economic factors
- Environmental factors
- Political/regulatory changes
- Industry dynamics

Generate 15-20 forces, then filter to most important.

**Step 3: Rank by Importance and Uncertainty** (20 min)

Plot forces on a matrix. Select 2 critical uncertainties (high importance + high uncertainty) for your scenario axes.

**Step 4: Build Scenario Frameworks** (30 min)

Example for a SaaS company:
- Uncertainty A: AI capability advancement (Slow ↔ Rapid)
- Uncertainty B: Economic environment (Recession ↔ Growth)

```
                    AI Advancement
                    Slow ←────────────→ Rapid
              ┌─────────────────┬─────────────────┐
    Growth    │   "Steady       │   "AI Boom"     │
              │   Climb"        │                 │
Economy       ├─────────────────┼─────────────────┤
              │   "Stagnation"  │   "Efficiency   │
    Recession │                 │   Imperative"   │
              └─────────────────┴─────────────────┘
```

**Step 5: Develop Scenario Narratives** (45 min)

For each scenario, write a 1-2 page story including:
- Vivid name that captures the essence
- How we got here (chain of events)
- What the world looks like
- Key characteristics and indicators
- Implications for your focal question

**Step 6: Test Strategies Against Scenarios** (30 min)

Strategy robustness matrix:

| Strategy | Scenario 1 | Scenario 2 | Scenario 3 | Scenario 4 |
|----------|------------|------------|------------|------------|
| Option A | Works well | Struggles | Works OK | Fails |
| Option B | Works OK | Works well | Works well | Works OK |

Option B is most robust—works reasonably across all futures.

**Step 7: Identify Early Warning Indicators** (15 min)

What signals would tell us which scenario is unfolding?

| Scenario | Early Indicators |
|----------|------------------|
| AI Boom | AI investment up 50%+ YoY, major product launches |
| Stagnation | Prolonged low growth, AI progress slows |

Monitor these to trigger strategy adjustments.

#### Common Mistakes

- **Forecasting disguised as scenarios** - Single "most likely" future isn't scenario planning
- **Best case / worst case** - Too simplistic, misses the nuance
- **Too many scenarios** - 4 is ideal, more than 6 is unusable
- **Correlated uncertainties** - If A causes B, they're not independent
- **No action implications** - Scenarios must connect to strategy

#### Tips

- **Choose orthogonal uncertainties** - Your two axes should be independent
- **Make scenarios vivid** - Names and narratives help people remember and use them
- **Avoid good/bad framing** - Each scenario should have opportunities and challenges
- **Include surprises** - What could happen that would catch everyone off-guard?
- **Revisit regularly** - Update scenarios as the future unfolds

### What-If Analysis

Rapidly explore how changes to key variables affect outcomes. Test assumptions, find sensitivities, and prepare for contingencies.

#### When to Use

- Testing assumptions in plans or models
- Finding which variables matter most
- Exploring edge cases
- Quick strategic exploration
- Preparing contingency plans
- **Time:** 15-45 minutes

#### Types of What-If Analysis

**Single Variable (Sensitivity)** - Change one variable, hold others constant.

Question format: "What if [variable] changes to [value]?"

Example:
- Base case: 1000 customers at $100/month = $100K MRR
- What if customers = 800? → $80K MRR (-20%)
- What if price = $120? → $120K MRR (+20%)

**Multi-Variable (Scenario)** - Change multiple variables together.

Question format: "What if [var1] AND [var2] change?"

Example:
- Pessimistic: 800 customers at $80 = $64K MRR (-36%)
- Optimistic: 1200 customers at $120 = $144K MRR (+44%)

**Extreme/Stress Test** - Push variables to extremes to find breaking points.

Question format: "What if [extreme situation]?"

Example:
- What if we lose our biggest customer (30% of revenue)?
- What if costs double overnight?
- What if market shrinks by 50%?

**Reversal** - Work backward from desired outcome.

Question format: "What would need to be true for [outcome]?"

Example:
- What would need to happen to double revenue?
- What would cause us to go bankrupt?

#### Process

**Step 1: Define Base Case** (5 min)

Document your current assumptions and expected outcomes.

**Step 2: Identify Key Variables** (5 min)

Categories:
- Revenue drivers (price, volume, conversion)
- Cost drivers (fixed, variable, headcount)
- Timing factors (launch date, sales cycle)
- External factors (market size, competition)
- Operational factors (capacity, quality)

**Step 3: Run What-If Scenarios** (15-30 min)

Sensitivity table:

| Variable | -50% | -20% | Base | +20% | +50% |
|----------|------|------|------|------|------|
| Price | $50 | $80 | $100 | $120 | $150 |
| Revenue impact | -50% | -20% | 0% | +20% | +50% |
| Customers | 500 | 800 | 1000 | 1200 | 1500 |
| Revenue impact | -50% | -20% | 0% | +20% | +50% |

**Step 4: Identify Sensitivities** (5 min)

Which variables have the biggest impact? Rank them. Focus planning and monitoring on high-sensitivity variables.

**Step 5: Develop Contingencies** (5 min)

| If... | Then... |
|-------|---------|
| Churn exceeds 8% | Trigger customer success intervention |
| Growth below 5% | Increase marketing spend or pivot |
| Costs exceed 90% of revenue | Implement cost reduction plan |

#### Quick What-If Questions

When you don't have time for full analysis:

- **Optimistic:** "What if everything goes 20% better than expected?"
- **Pessimistic:** "What if everything goes 20% worse?"
- **Single failure:** "What if [biggest assumption] is wrong?"
- **Timing:** "What if this takes twice as long?"
- **Scale:** "What if we need to 10x this?"
- **Constraint:** "What if [key resource] is unavailable?"

#### Common Mistakes

- **Only testing favorable scenarios** - Test the uncomfortable ones too
- **Ignoring correlations** - Variables often move together (recession = lower volume AND lower prices)
- **False precision** - "Revenue will be $1,234,567"—use ranges
- **Not testing assumptions** - The base case itself might be wrong
- **Analysis without action** - What-if should lead to contingency plans

#### Tips

- **Test non-obvious variables** - Don't just test price and volume. Test timing, quality, assumptions about customer behavior.
- **Use ranges, not points** - "What if price is $80-120?" is more useful than "What if price is $97?"
- **Look for non-linear effects** - Some variables have thresholds. Going from 5% churn to 6% might be fine; 5% to 15% might be catastrophic.
- **Document assumptions** - Every what-if depends on assumptions. Make them explicit.
